## Plain English

Egypt charges household electricity in steps — the first slice of the month is cheap, the next slice is a bit more expensive, and the very last slice costs almost three times the first.
Until today, the page told the homeowner one big yearly savings figure, but never showed *which* steps the savings came from, even though that is the whole reason rooftop solar is more valuable in Egypt than a calculator pretending the price is flat would suggest.
Today we added a new picture that paints the bill as a stack of those steps — the cheap step at the bottom, the expensive step on top — and shows two of those stacks side by side, one for the bill before solar and one for the bill after.
You can see at a glance that the right-hand stack is much shorter than the left, and that the shrinking is concentrated at the top: solar is shaving the priciest layers off first, exactly the layers a flat-tariff calculator would have invisibly under-counted.
A small caption at the top of the picture spells the headline out — total yearly savings and the share of those savings that came off the most expensive step the household actually used.

- `frontend/src/components/charts/TierBracketChart.tsx` — Recharts `BarChart` with two side-by-side stacked bars ("Without solar" / "With solar"). - `frontend/src/components/charts/TierBracketChart.test.tsx` — six vitest cases covering the sr-only fallback table, the chart container's aria-label, the Know-more wiring, the savings-and-top-tier caption (asserted at the exact arithmetic the EgyptERA marginal kernel would produce on the fixture), the schedule-label fallback, and the no-PV degenerate case where the caption is hidden.
- `frontend/src/content/explainers.ts` — new `tier-bracket-savings` Know-more entry: plain-English explanation of progressive marginal billing and why solar erodes the top tier first, the formula block, the actual EgyptERA tier prices and ratios, and source links.
- `frontend/src/components/dashboard/Dashboard.tsx` — mounts the new `tier-bracket-section` once an estimate has succeeded; carries an inline `EGYPT_TARIFF_TIER_LABELS` constant (mirror of the backend's `EGYPT_RESIDENTIAL_TARIFF_TIERS`) so the chart can label each layer with its kWh band and price without round-tripping the schedule through the wire.
- `frontend/src/components/dashboard/Dashboard.test.tsx` — fixture reshape: the dashboard mock's `monthly_bill_before` / `monthly_bill_after` previously held empty arrays (Day 14 didn't yet need them); they now carry economically realistic per-tier breakdowns generated by an in-test mirror of the backend kernel. Updates the existing annual-savings assertion to the new fixture's number, plus three new assertions that pin the tier-bracket section is mounted on success and hidden in the placeholder/error states.

The dashboard data orchestrator (`useDashboardEstimate`) is **untouched** — the per-tier breakdowns ride on the `/api/tariff/savings` response that the Day-14 dashboard has consumed since the day the headline savings card landed, so no extra round-trip was added.

## Why this matters (academic logic)

Contribution B of the thesis is "Egypt's tiered tariff systematically changes the math, and a flat-tariff calculator under-counts payback." The Day-8 kernel proved the claim numerically; the Day-14 dashboard surfaced the numerical *consequence* (the headline EGP/yr savings card and its `tiered-tariff` Know-more); the present surface is what the contribution actually *looks* like to the reader.

1. **Why a stacked bar instead of two side-by-side grouped bars per tier?** Grouped bars preserve the per-tier comparison but lose the totals — a reader cannot read the overall bill height directly, which is the headline number. Stacked bars carry both: the total bill is the height of the bar, and each layer is the contribution of one tier. The price the homeowner pays attention to is the *bill*, so the bill height has to be the dominant visual.

2. **Why tier order bottom-to-top from cheapest to priciest, rather than the other way around?** The contribution's narrative sentence is "PV erases the top tier first." Reading the chart should reproduce that sentence: the priciest tier sits at the top of the stack, where the eye lands; the right-hand bar is missing exactly that top region. Inverting the order would still be mathematically correct but would tell the *opposite* visual story — that PV erases the cheap consumption and leaves the expensive part untouched.

3. **Why split by EGP rather than by kWh?** Both decompositions live in the sr-only table for completeness, but the visible chart is in EGP because the homeowner's question is "how much money does each tier cost me, and how much of *that* does solar take?". A kWh decomposition would tell the same story qualitatively (the top kWh band shrinks the most) but would under-state the *value* of that shrinkage, since one kWh saved at 1.55 EGP/kWh is worth ~2.7× one kWh saved at 0.58 EGP/kWh. EGP is the unit that makes Contribution B's claim quantitative.

4. **Why annual aggregation rather than per-month bars?** Per-month bars would render 12×7 = 84 segments for the without-solar case alone; the chart would be illegible. An annual aggregate is the right granularity for the headline contribution claim — payback calculations are annual, EgyptERA reform cycles are annual, and the homeowner's mental model of "my bill" is annual. The monthly granularity is preserved end-to-end in the underlying data and could be exposed as a per-month sub-view if the methodology section needs it.

5. **Why "active top tier" rather than the schedule's `> 1,000 kWh` tail in the caption?** A 500-kWh/month household never reaches the 1.55 EGP tail tier, so the schedule's absolute top tier is both 0 EGP before *and* 0 EGP after — the share-of-savings denominator would yield a misleading "0%". The right thing for the homeowner-facing caption is the highest tier the household *actually uses* — the tier the reader's eye is drawn to as the topmost coloured layer in the without-solar bar. The chart computes this dynamically, falling back to the schedule top only for a zero-bill household.

6. **Why mirror the EgyptERA tier prices into a frontend constant rather than fetching them?** The schedule is an Act-of-EgyptERA constant within a reform cycle, not a per-request value, and the caller is rendering them solely as labels (`"50–100 kWh @ 0.68"`). Fetching them would add a network round-trip, an additional failure mode, and a stale-cache problem — for zero gain. When the backend grows a tariff-schedule endpoint we will point at it; for now the constant is a documented mirror with a single source of truth (`backend/app/config.py::EGYPT_RESIDENTIAL_TARIFF_TIERS`).

7. **Why no backend change at all on Day 17?** The Day-8 schemas (`MonthlyBillBreakdown.per_tier_kwh`, `per_tier_egp`) were specifically designed with this chart in mind — the docstring on `MonthlyBillBreakdown` says so. Touching the backend now would either add a parallel "tier-bracket aggregate" endpoint (duplicating data the existing endpoint already returns) or change the wire shape (a destabilising churn for a UI-only deliverable). Aggregating client-side is the correct seam: the kernel returns the maximally-decomposed view and the dashboard chooses what to display.

8. **Why a fallback `<table>` for both kWh and EGP rather than just one?** The chart's visible surface is EGP-only by design (point 3), but a screen-reader user cannot toggle between visual encodings — the table has to carry the full information so the chart's visual choice does not amount to discrimination. Six columns (tier, before-kWh, before-EGP, after-kWh, after-EGP, saved-EGP) plus a totals row gives the same homeowner-and-examiner-grade view a sighted reader gets, plus the per-tier kWh aggregation that the visual chart elides.
